**SMS _Friedrich der Grosse_** ("His Majesty's Ship _Frederick the Great_")[[a]][17] 
was the second vessel of the [_Kaiser_ class][14] of [battleships][15] of the 
German [Imperial Navy][18]. _Friedrich der Grosse_'s[[b]][19] keel was laid 
on 26 January 1910 at the [AG Vulcan][20] dockyard in [Hamburg][11], her hull 
was launched on 10 June 1911, and she was commissioned into the fleet on 15 
October 1912. The ship was equipped with ten 30.5-centimeter (12.0 in) guns 
in five twin turrets, and had a top speed of 23.4 knots (43.3 km/h; 26.9 mph). 
_Friedrich der Grosse_ was assigned to the III Squadron of the [High Seas Fleet][21] 
for the majority of [World War I][22], and served as fleet [flagship][23] from 
her commissioning until 1917.[[2]][24]

Along with her four sister ships, [_Kaiser_][25], [_Kaiserin_][26], [_König 
Albert_][27], and [_Prinzregent Luitpold_][28], _Friedrich der Grosse_ participated 
in all the major fleet operations of World War I, including the [Battle of 
Jutland][29] on 31 May – 1 June 1916. Toward the center of the German line, 
_Friedrich der Grosse_ was not as heavily engaged as the leading German ships, 
such as the battleships [_König_][30] and [_Grosser Kurfürst_][31] and the 
battlecruisers of the I Scouting Group—_Friedrich der Grosse_ emerged from 
the battle completely unscathed. In 1917, the new battleship [_Baden_][32] 
replaced _Friedrich der Grosse_ as the fleet flagship.

After Germany's defeat in the war and the signing of the [Armistice][33] in 
November 1918, _Friedrich der Grosse_ and most of the [capital ships][34] of 
the High Seas Fleet were interned by the British [Royal Navy][35] in [Scapa 
Flow][13]. The ships were disarmed and reduced to skeleton crews while the 
[Allied powers][36] negotiated the final version of the [Treaty of Versailles][37]. 
On 21 June 1919, days before the treaty was signed, the commander of the interned 
fleet, Rear Admiral [Ludwig von Reuter][38], [ordered the fleet to be scuttled][39] 
to ensure that the British would not be able to seize the ships. _Friedrich 
der Grosse_ was raised in 1936 and broken up for scrap metal. Her bell was 
returned to Germany in 1965 and is in the Fleet Headquarters in [Glücksburg][40].

## 
[[edit][53]] Construction

[![A large warship with five gun turrets, two tall masts, two funnels, and heavy armor protection.][54]][55]

[![][56]][55]

 The shaded areas represent the portions of the ship protected by armor

Ordered under the contract name _Ersatz Heimdall_ as a replacement for the 
obsolete [coastal defense ship][57] [_Heimdall_][58],[[3]][59][[c]][60]_Friedrich 
der Grosse_ was laid down at the [AG Vulcan][20] dockyard in [Hamburg][11] 
on 26 January 1910.[[4]][61] She was launched on 10 June 1911, after which 
AG Vulcan conducted builder's trials.[[2]][24] At her launching ceremony, [Princess 
Alexandra Victoria][62] performed the christening and Field Marshal [Colmar 
Freiherr von der Goltz][63] gave a speech.[[5]][64] She was then transferred 
to [Wilhelmshaven][65] and commissioned into the fleet on 15 October 1912. 
Exercises in the [Baltic Sea][66] followed; _Friedrich der Grosse_ then went 
to [Kiel][67] for final [fitting-out][68] work. On 22 January 1913, the ship 
was finally ready for active service.[[2]][24] She cost the German government 
45,802,000 [marks][69].[[1]][16]

_Friedrich der Grosse_ was 172.4 m (566 ft) long [overall][70] and displaced 
a maximum of 27,000 metric tons (27,000 long tons; 30,000 short tons). The 
ship had a beam of 29 m (95 ft) and a draft of 9.1 m (30 ft) forward and 8.8 m 
(29 ft) aft. She was powered by three sets of AEG Curtis [turbines][71], supplied 
with steam by 16 coal-fired [boilers][72]. On trials, the powerplant produced 
a top speed of 22.4 [knots][73] (41.5 km/h; 25.8 mph). She carried 3,600 metric 
tons (3,500 long tons; 4,000 short tons) of coal, which enabled a maximum range 
of 7,900 [nautical miles][74] (14,600 km; 9,100 mi) at a cruising speed of 
12 kn (22 km/h; 14 mph). _Friedrich der Grosse_ was protected by heavy [Krupp 
cemented armor][75]; the main [armored belt][76] was 350 mm (14 in) thick [amidships][77] 
and the [conning tower][78] had 400 mm (16 in) thick sides. The [gun turrets][79] 
had 300 mm (12 in) sides. As with the other four ships in her class, _Friedrich 
der Grosse_ carried [anti-torpedo nets][80] until after the [Battle of Jutland][29] 
in 1916.[[1]][16]

The ship was armed with a main battery of ten [30.5 cm SK L/50 guns][81] in 
five twin turrets.[[1]][16] She disposed with the inefficient hexagonal turret 
arrangement of previous German battleships; instead, three of the five turrets 
were mounted on the centerline, with two of them arranged in a [superfiring 
pair][82] aft. The other two turrets were placed [_en echelon_][83] amidships, 
such that both could fire on the [broadside][84].[[6]][85] The ship was also 
armed with fourteen [15 cm (5.9 in) SK L/45 guns][86] in [casemates][87] amidships, 
eight 8.8 cm (3.5 in) SK L/45 guns in casemates; these were removed during 
the war and replaced with four 8.8 cm L/45 anti-aircraft guns. Her armament 
was rounded out by five 50 cm (20 in) torpedo tubes, all mounted in the ship's 
hull.[[1]][16]

## 
[[edit][88]] Service history

After her commissioning in January 1913, _Friedrich der Grosse_ conducted [sea 
trials][89] before becoming the fleet [flagship][23] on 2 March,[[2]][24] replacing 
[_Deutschland_][90].[[7]][91] The ship participated in her first round of fleet 
maneuvers in February 1913, which were conducted in the [Kattegat][92] and 
the North Sea. The next month saw another round of exercises, from 12 to 14 
March. The ship went into dock for periodic maintenance in April, and was ready 
for artillery training by the end of the month. Extensive fleet maneuvers were 
conducted in the North Sea from 5 to 27 May. _Friedrich der Grosse_, as the 
Navy's newest battleship, was sent to Kiel for [Kiel Week][93] in June.[[2]][24] 
While there, she was visited by the Italian king [Victor Emmanuel III][94] 
and his wife [Elena][95].[[5]][64] In mid-July, the fleet conducted its annual 
summer cruise to Norway, which lasted until mid-August. During the cruise, 
_Friedrich der Grosse_ visited [Balholmen][96], Norway. The autumn maneuvers 
followed after the fleet returned; they lasted from 31 August to 9 September. 
Unit drills and individual ship training were conducted in October and November.[[2]][24] 

In early 1914, _Friedrich der Grosse_ participated in additional ship and unit 
training. The annual spring maneuvers were conducted in the North Sea at the 
end of March. Further fleet exercises followed in April and May in the Baltic 
and North Seas. The ship again went to Kiel Week that year. Despite the rising 
international tensions following the [assassination][97] of [Archduke Franz 
Ferdinand][98] on 28 June, the [High Seas Fleet][21] began its summer cruise 
to Norway on 13 July. During the last peacetime cruise of the Imperial Navy, 
the fleet conducted drills off [Skagen][99] before proceeding to the Norwegian 
fjords on 25 July. The following day the fleet began to steam back to Germany, 
as a result of [Austria-Hungary][100]'s [ultimatum to Serbia][101]. On the 
27th, the entire fleet assembled off [Cape Skudenes][102] before returning 
to port, where they remained at a heightened state of readiness.[[2]][24] War 
between Austria-Hungary and Serbia broke out the following day, and in the 
span of a week all of the major European powers had joined the conflict.[[8]][103] 

### 
[[edit][104]] World War I

The High Seas Fleet, including _Friedrich der Grosse_, conducted a number of 
sweeps and advances into the North Sea. The first occurred on 2–3 November 
1914, though no British forces were encountered. Admiral [Friedrich von Ingenohl][105], 
the commander of the High Seas Fleet, adopted a strategy in which the battlecruisers 
of Rear Admiral [Franz von Hipper][106]'s [I Scouting Group][107] raided British 
coastal towns to lure out portions of the [Grand Fleet][108] where they could 
be destroyed by the High Seas Fleet.[[9]][109] The [raid on Scarborough, Hartlepool 
and Whitby][110] on 15–16 December 1914 was the first such operation.[[10]][111] 
On the evening of 15 December, the German battle fleet of some twelve dreadnoughts—including 
_Friedrich der Grosse_ and her four [sisters][112]—and eight pre-dreadnoughts 
came to within 10 nmi (19 km; 12 mi) of an isolated [squadron][113] of six 
British battleships. Skirmishes between the rival [destroyer][114] screens 
in the darkness convinced von Ingenohl that he was faced with the entire [Grand 
Fleet][108]. Under orders from [Kaiser Wilhelm II][115] to avoid risking the 
fleet unnecessarily, von Ingenohl broke off the engagement and turned the battle 
fleet back toward Germany.[[11]][116]

[![][117]][118]

[![][56]][118]

_Friedrich der Grosse_ sometime before 1917

Following the loss of [SMS _Blücher_][119] at the [Battle of Dogger Bank][120] 
in January 1915, the Kaiser removed Admiral von Ingenohl from his post on 2 
February. Admiral [Hugo von Pohl][121] replaced him as commander of the fleet.[[12]][122] 
Admiral von Pohl conducted a series of fleet advances in 1915 in which _Friedrich 
der Grosse_ took part; in the first one on 29–30 March, the fleet steamed out 
to the north of [Terschelling][123] and return without incident. Another followed 
on 17–18 April, where _Friedrich der Grosse_ and the rest of the fleet covered 
a mining operation by the II Scouting Group. Three days later, on 21–22 April, 
the High Seas Fleet advanced toward [Dogger Bank][124], though again failed 
to meet any British forces.[[13]][125]

The II Scouting Group performed another minelaying operation on 17–18 May, 
and _Friedrich der Grosse_ and the rest of the fleet steamed out in support. 
Less than two weeks later on 29–30 May, the fleet attempted to conduct a sweep 
in the North Sea, but inclement weather forced Pohl to cancel the operation 
some 50 nmi (93 km; 58 mi) off [Schiermonnikoog][126]. The fleet remained in 
port until 10 August, when it sortied to [Helgoland][127] to cover the return 
of the [auxiliary cruiser][128] [_Möwe_][129]. A month later, on 11–12 September, 
the fleet covered another mine-laying operation off the [Swarte Bank][130]. 
The last operation of the year, conducted on 23–24 October, was an advance 
without result in the direction of [Horns Reef][131].[[13]][125]

On 11 January 1916, Admiral [Reinhard Scheer][132] replaced von Pohl, who was 
suffering from liver cancer.[[14]][133] A week later on the 18th, Scheer hoisted 
his flag aboard _Friedrich der Grosse_.[[13]][125] Scheer proposed a more aggressive 
policy designed to force a confrontation with the British [Grand Fleet][108]; 
he received approval from the Kaiser in February.[[15]][134] The first of Scheer's 
operations was conducted the following month, on 5–7 March, with an uneventful 
sweep of the [Hoofden][135].[[16]][136] On 25–26 March, Scheer attempted to 
attack British forces that had raided [Tondern][137], but failed to locate 
them. Another advance to Horns Reef followed on 21–22 April.[[13]][125]

On 24 April, the [battlecruisers][138] of Von Hipper's I Scouting Group conducted 
a [raid on the English coast][139]. _Friedrich der Grosse_ and the rest of 
the fleet sailed in distant support. The battlecruiser [_Seydlitz_][140] struck 
a mine while en route to the target, and had to withdraw.[[17]][141] The other 
battlecruisers bombarded the town of Lowestoft unopposed but, during the approach 
to Yarmouth, they encountered the British cruisers of the [Harwich Force][142]. 
A short artillery duel ensued before the Harwich Force withdrew. Reports of 
British submarines in the area prompted the retreat of the I Scouting Group. 
At this point, Scheer, who had been warned of the sortie of the Grand Fleet 
from its base in Scapa Flow, also withdrew to safer German waters.[[18]][143] 

#### 
[[edit][144]] Battle of Jutland

Main article: [Battle of Jutland][29]

[![][145]][146]

[![][56]][146]

 Maps showing the maneuvers of the British (blue) and German (red) fleets on 31 May – 1 June 1916; [click][147] for a larger view.

Soon after the Lowestoft raid, Scheer began planning another foray into the 
North Sea. He had initially intended to launch the operation in mid-May, by 
which time the mine damage to _Seydlitz_ was scheduled to be repaired—Scheer 
was unwilling to embark on a major raid without his battlecruiser forces at 
full strength. On 9 May, several battleships developed problems with their 
engines, which delayed the operation further, to 23 May.[[19]][148] By 22 May, 
_Seydlitz_ was still not fully repaired and the operation was again postponed, 
to 29 May.[[20]][149] At noon on 29 May, the repairs to _Seydlitz_ were finally 
completed, and the ship returned to the I Scouting Group.[[21]][150] The plan 
called for Hipper's battlecruisers to steam north to the [Skagerrak][151], 
with the intention of luring out a portion of the British fleet so it could 
be destroyed by Scheer's waiting battleships.[[22]][152]

_Friedrich der Grosse_ was the eighth ship in the German line; the four [_König_-class][153] 
ships of the V Division, III Battle Squadron, led the line, followed by four 
_Kaiser_-class ships in the VI Division, III Battle Squadron. _Friedrich der 
Grosse_ was the last ship in her division, directly astern of [_Prinzregent 
Luitpold_][28] and ahead of [_Ostfriesland_][154], the flagship of Vice Admiral 
Schmidt's I Division, I Battle Squadron. The eight [_Helgoland_][155] and [_Nassau_][156] 
classes constituted the I Squadron, which was followed by the six elderly [pre-dreadnoughts][157] 
of Rear Admiral [Franz Mauve][158]'s II Squadron.[[23]][159] Hipper's five 
battlecruisers, the scouting force for the fleet, left the [Jade estuary][160] 
at 02:00 on 31 May; Scheer and the High Seas Fleet followed an hour and a half 
later.[[21]][150]

Shortly before 16:00 the battlecruisers of [I Scouting Group][107] encountered 
the British 1st Battlecruiser Squadron under the command of Vice Admiral [Sir 
David Beatty][161]. The opposing ships began an artillery duel that saw the 
destruction of [_Indefatigable_][162], shortly after 17:00,[[24]][163] and 
[_Queen Mary_][164], less than half an hour later.[[25]][165] By this time, 
the German battlecruisers were steaming south to draw the British ships toward 
the main body of the High Seas Fleet. At 17:30, _König_'s crew spotted both 
the I Scouting Group and the 1st Battlecruiser Squadron approaching. The German 
battlecruisers were steaming to starboard, while the British ships steamed 
to port. At 17:45, Scheer ordered a [two-point][166] turn to port to bring 
his ships closer to the British battlecruisers and the accompanying fast battleships 
of the 5th Battle Squadron; a minute later, he gave the order to open fire.[[26]][167][[d]][168] 

_Friedrich der Grosse_ was still out of range of both the British battlecruisers 
and the 5th Battle Squadron, and so held her fire initially. Between 17:48 
and 17:52, _Friedrich der Grosse_ and ten other battleships engaged the 2nd 
Light Cruiser Squadron, though only [_Nassau_][169] managed to score a hit 
during this period.[[27]][170] Shortly after, the German battle line came across 
the disabled destroyers [_Nestor_][171] and [_Nomad_][172]. _Friedrich der 
Grosse_ and her three sisters targeted _Nomad_ and quickly sank her. _Nestor_ 
was similarly dispatched by the I Squadron ships.[[28]][173] Shortly after 
19:00, a melee between the German line and British cruisers took place. The 
center of the action was the damaged German cruiser [_Wiesbaden_][174], which 
had been disabled by a shell from the British battlecruiser [_Invincible_][175]. 
Rear Admiral Behncke in _König_ attempted to maneuver the III Squadron to cover 
the stricken cruiser.[[29]][176] Simultaneously, the British 3rd and 4th Light 
Cruiser Squadrons began a torpedo attack on the German line; while advancing 
to torpedo range, they smothered _Wiesbaden_ with fire from their main guns. 
The eight III Squadron battleships fired on the British cruisers, but even 
the sustained fire from the battleships' main guns failed to drive off the 
British cruisers.[[30]][177] The armored cruisers [_Defence_][178], [_Warrior_][179], 
and [_Black Prince_][180] joined in the attack on the crippled _Wiesbaden_.[[31]][181] 
While most of the III Squadron battleships rained heavy fire upon the attacking 
armored cruisers, _Friedrich der Grosse_ and the I Squadron ships engaged the 
battleship [_Warspite_][182] at ranges from 9,600 to 12,500 yd (8,800 to 11,400 
m), until _Warspite_ disappeared in the haze. In this period, _Warspite_ was 
hit by 13 heavy shells, though the ships that fired them are unknown.[[32]][183] 

After successfully withdrawing from the British, Scheer ordered the fleet to 
assume night cruising formation, though communication errors between Scheer 
aboard _Friedrich der Grosse_ and [_Westfalen_][184], the lead ship, caused 
delays. The series of reversals in course and confused maneuvers disorganized 
the fleet and inverted the sequence of ships, but by 23:30 the fleet had reached 
its cruising formation. _Friedrich der Grosse_ was now the ninth ship in a 
line of twenty-four, headed by the eight I Squadron ships.[[33]][185] Shortly 
after 01:00, the British cruiser [_Black Prince_][180] stumbled into the German 
line. Searchlights aboard [_Thüringen_][186] illuminated the target; _Friedrich 
der Grosse_, _Thüringen_, [_Nassau_][169], and [_Ostfriesland_][154] hammered 
the cruiser at point-blank range with main and secondary guns. In the span 
of a few minutes _Black Prince_ exploded and sank, taking her entire crew of 
857 with her.[[34]][187]

After a series of night engagements between the I Squadron battleships and 
British destroyers, the High Seas Fleet punched through the British light forces 
and reached [Horns Reef][131] by 04:00 on 1 June.[[35]][188] The German fleet 
reached Wilhelmshaven a few hours later; five of the I Squadron battleships 
took up defensive positions in the outer [roadstead][189], and _Kaiser_, _Kaiserin_, 
_Prinzregent Luitpold_, and _Kronprinz_ stood ready just outside the entrance 
to Wilhelmshaven.[[36]][190] The rest of the fleet entered Wilhelmshaven, where 
_Friedrich der Grosse_ and the other ships still in fighting condition replenished 
their stocks of coal and ammunition.[[37]][191] In the course of the battle, 
_Friedrich der Grosse_ had fired 72 main battery shells and 151 rounds from 
her secondary guns.[[38]][192] She emerged from the battle completely undamaged.[[13]][125] 

#### 
[[edit][193]] Subsequent North Sea operations

On 18 August 1916, _Friedrich der Grosse_ took part in an operation to bombard 
[Sunderland][194].[[13]][125] Admiral Scheer attempted a repeat of the original 
31 May plan: the two serviceable German battlecruisers, [_Moltke_][195] and 
[_Von der Tann_][196], augmented by three faster dreadnoughts, were to bombard 
the coastal town of Sunderland in an attempt to draw out and destroy Beatty's 
battlecruisers. Scheer, in _Friedrich der Grosse_, would trail behind with 
the rest of the fleet and provide support.[[39]][197] Scheer turned north after 
receiving a false report from a [zeppelin][198] about a British unit in the 
area.[[13]][125] As a result, the bombardment was not carried out, and by 14:35, 
Scheer had been warned of the Grand Fleet's approach and so turned his forces 
around and retreated to German ports.[[40]][199]

Another fleet operation took place on 18–19 October, though it ended without 
encountering any British units. The fleet was reorganized on 1 December;[[13]][125] 
the four _König_-class battleships remained in III Squadron, along with the 
newly commissioned [_Bayern_][200], while the five _Kaiser_-class ships, including 
_Friedrich der Grosse_, were transferred to IV Squadron.[[41]][201] In March 
1917 the new battleship [_Baden_][32], built to serve as fleet flagship, entered 
service;[[42]][202] on the 17th, Scheer hauled down his flag from _Friedrich 
der Grosse_ and transferred it to _Baden_.[[13]][125] On 4–5 July, crewmen 
aboard _Friedrich der Grosse_ staged a hunger strike in protest over the poor 
quality and insufficient quantity of the food they were given. The ship's officers 
relented, fed the crew a meal of [groat][203] soup and agreed to form a _Menagekommission_, 
a council that gave the enlisted men a voice in their ration selection and 
preparation.[[43]][204] Further insubordination on several vessels followed 
on 11 July, and the ringleaders were arrested and put on trial. [Max Reichpietsch][205], 
a stoker from _Friedrich der Grosse_ was sentenced to death and executed by 
firing squad on 5 September in [Cologne][206].[[44]][207][[45]][208]

#### 
[[edit][209]] Operation Albion

Main article: [Operation Albion][210]

[![A small boat packed with soldiers passes in front of a cruiser and several transport ships][211]][212]

[![][56]][212]

 German troops landing at Ösel

In early September 1917, following the German conquest of the Russian port 
of [Riga][213], the German navy decided to eliminate the Russian naval forces 
that still held the [Gulf of Riga][214]. The _Admiralstab_ (Navy High Command) 
planned an operation to seize the Baltic island of [Ösel][215], and specifically 
the Russian gun batteries on the [Sworbe Peninsula][216].[[46]][217] The _Admiralstab_ 
issued orders on 18 September for a joint operation with the army to capture 
Ösel and [Moon][218] Islands; the primary naval component was to comprise the 
flagship, _Moltke_, and the III and IV Battle Squadrons of the High Seas Fleet. 
Along with nine light cruisers, three torpedo boat flotillas, and dozens of 
[mine][219] warfare ships, the entire force numbered some 300 ships, supported 
by over 100 aircraft and six zeppelins. The invasion force amounted to approximately 
24,600 officers and enlisted men.[[47]][220] Opposing the Germans were the 
old Russian [pre-dreadnoughts][221] [_Slava_][222] and [_Tsarevitch_][223], 
the [armored cruisers][224] _Bayan_, _Admiral Makarov_, and _Diana_, 26 destroyers, 
and several torpedo boats and gunboats. The garrison on Ösel numbered some 
14,000 men.[[48]][225]

The operation began on the morning of 12 October, when _Moltke_ and the III 
Squadron ships engaged Russian positions in Tagga Bay while _Friedrich der 
Grosse_ and the rest of IV Squadron shelled Russian gun batteries on the [Sworbe 
Peninsula][226] on [Ösel][215].[[48]][225] The coastal artillery in both locations 
were quickly silenced by the battleships' heavy guns.[[49]][227] On the morning 
of the 14th, _Friedrich der Grosse_, _Kaiserin_, and _König Albert_ were detached 
to support German troops advancing toward [Anseküll][228].[[50]][229] The three 
ships fired on the battery at [Zerel][230] for an hour, which prompted most 
of the Russian gun crews to flee their posts.[[51]][231]

By 20 October, the fighting on the islands was winding down; Moon, Ösel, and 
Dagö were in German possession. The previous day, the _Admiralstab_ had ordered 
the cessation of naval actions and the return of the dreadnoughts to the High 
Seas Fleet as soon as possible.[[52]][232] On 27 October, _Friedrich der Grosse_ 
was detached from the Baltic and returned to the North Sea. Upon her return, 
she resumed guard duties. The ship participated in the fruitless advance to 
Norway on 23–25 April 1918. Afterward, she went into drydock for extensive 
maintenance, which lasted from 26 July to 28 September.[[44]][207]

### 
[[edit][233]] Fate

Main article: [Scuttling of the German fleet in Scapa Flow][39]

_Friedrich der Grosse_ and her four sisters were to have taken part in a final 
fleet action at the end of October 1918, days before the [Armistice][33] was 
to take effect. The bulk of the High Seas Fleet was to have sortied from their 
base in Wilhelmshaven to engage the British Grand Fleet; Scheer—by now the 
_Grossadmiral_ ([Grand Admiral][234]) of the fleet—intended to inflict as much 
damage as possible on the British navy, to ensure a better bargaining position 
for Germany, despite the expected casualties. However, many of the war-weary 
sailors felt the operation would disrupt the peace process and prolong the 
war.[[53]][235] On the morning of 29 October 1918, the order was given to sail 
from Wilhelmshaven the following day. Starting on the night of 29 October, 
sailors on [_Thüringen_][186] and then on several other battleships [mutinied][236].[[54]][237] 
On the 30th, crewmen aboard _Friedrich der Grosse_ engaged in forms of passive 
resistance, including a "go-slow" while replenishing the ship's coal stock.[[44]][207][[e]][238] 
The unrest ultimately forced Hipper and Scheer to cancel the operation.[[55]][239] 
Informed of the situation, the Kaiser stated "I no longer have a navy."[[56]][240] 

[![A map designating the locations where the German ships were sunk.][241]][242]

[![][56]][243]

 Map of the scuttled ships showing _Friedrich der Grosse_ (#2); [click][244] for a larger view

Following the capitulation of Germany in November 1918, most of the High Seas 
Fleet, under the command of Rear Admiral [Ludwig von Reuter][38], were interned 
in the British naval base in [Scapa Flow][13].[[55]][239] Prior to the departure 
of the German fleet, Admiral [Adolf von Trotha][245] made clear to Von Reuter 
that he could not allow the Allies to seize the ships, under any conditions.[[57]][246] 
The fleet rendezvoused with the British [light cruiser][247] [_Cardiff_][248], 
which led the ships to the Allied fleet that was to escort the Germans to Scapa 
Flow. The massive flotilla consisted of some 370 British, American, and French 
warships.[[58]][249] Once the ships were interned, their guns were disabled 
through the removal of their [breech blocks][250], and their crews were reduced 
to 200 officers and enlisted men.[[59]][251]

The fleet remained in captivity during the negotiations that ultimately produced 
the [Treaty of Versailles][37]. Von Reuter believed that the British intended 
to seize the German ships on 21 June 1919, which was the deadline for Germany 
to sign the peace treaty. Unaware that the deadline had been extended to the 
23rd, Reuter [ordered the ships to be sunk][39] at the next opportunity. On 
the morning of 21 June, the British fleet left Scapa Flow to conduct training 
maneuvers, and at 11:20 Reuter transmitted the order to his ships.[[57]][246]_Friedrich 
der Grosse_ capsized and sank at 12:16. In 1936, the ship was raised and broken 
up for scrap; the work was completed by 29 April 1937. On 30 August 1965,[[1]][16] 
Britain returned the ship's bell to Germany via the frigate [_Scheer_][252];[[60]][253] 
it now resides at the Fleet Headquarters of the [German Navy][254] in [Glücksburg][40].[[1]][16]
